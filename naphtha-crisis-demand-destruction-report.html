1. Executive Summary

$1,000/t Naphtha price (late March)
2.8–4M b/d Asia refinery run cuts (Mar–Apr)
1.3M b/d China crude demand reduction
50–60% Asian plant utilisation rate

On February 28, 2026, the Strait of Hormuz was effectively blockaded, severing approximately 30% of global naphtha supply. Within weeks, naphtha prices surged 60% to $1,000/tonne, triggering a cascade of petrochemical plant shutdowns across Asia. China — the world's largest petrochemical consumer — saw confirmed facility closures, force majeures, and widespread run cuts that collectively removed an estimated 1.3 million barrels per day of crude oil demand.[1][2]

This report traces the chain of events from the initial supply disruption through to its impact on plastic prices, petrochemical capacity, and — critically — global oil demand destruction. The story here is not just about naphtha. It is about how a single chokepoint failure can propagate through the entire hydrocarbon value chain, creating a measurable hole in global crude demand at precisely the moment geopolitical risk should be pushing prices higher.

2. The Trigger: Strait of Hormuz Blockade

Key fact: ~30% of global seaborne naphtha trade transits the Strait of Hormuz. When it closed on February 28, 2026, the world's petrochemical industry lost access to its single largest feedstock corridor overnight.[1]

The Strait of Hormuz connects the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman and the open ocean. It is the world's most critical oil chokepoint, with roughly 20 million barrels per day of crude and petroleum products flowing through it under normal conditions. For naphtha specifically — the light hydrocarbon fraction used as the primary feedstock for ethylene crackers across Asia — the strait's closure was catastrophic.[1]

Middle Eastern producers (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Iran) are the dominant suppliers of naphtha to Asian petrochemical complexes. Japan, South Korea, and China are the three largest importers. When the strait closed, these importers faced an immediate and severe feedstock deficit with no realistic short-term alternatives at comparable cost or volume.[1][3]

3. Naphtha Price Shock

Naphtha Price Trajectory (Feb–Apr 2026)

Source: C&EN, Hydrocarbon Processing, ICIS pricing data[1][5]

Naphtha prices surged approximately 60% in one month following the blockade. The CFR Japan benchmark — the reference price for Asian naphtha imports — moved from roughly $625/tonne in mid-February to $1,000/tonne by late March 2026.[1]

This price move is not just expensive — it breaks the economics of naphtha-based crackers entirely. At $1,000/tonne, most Asian ethylene crackers operate at negative margins. The choice becomes simple: shut down or bleed cash. Most chose to shut down.[1][5]

Bloomberg (April 15): China's petrochemical producers are "broadly idling." Across Asia, plants are running at 50–60% utilisation, with 10+ plants fully halted.[4]

5. The Broader Petrochemical Restructuring

The Hormuz shock is not hitting a healthy industry. It is accelerating a structural decline that was already underway.

East Asian Ethylene Capacity Retirements (Planned & Accelerated)

Source: C&EN, NDRC policy documents[1][6]

China's National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC) had already earmarked approximately 7.4 million tonnes per year of ethylene cracking capacity for closure by 2027 — targeting plants older than 20 years. Combined with similar rationalisation in Japan (~3M tpy) and South Korea (~2.6M tpy), East Asia is undergoing its largest petrochemical capacity restructuring in decades: roughly 13 million tonnes per year of ethylene capacity being retired.[1][6]

The Hormuz crisis is collapsing this multi-year timeline into months. Plants that had years of remaining operational life are finding the economics of running on $1,000/tonne naphtha impossible. The "planned" transition is becoming a forced one.[6]

Polypropylene is the critical shortage. It goes into food packaging, containers, medical supplies, automotive parts, and textiles. When PP supply drops, it ripples through consumer-facing industries immediately. A German packaging industry survey found 99% of manufacturers were facing supplier price increases, with most unable to pass costs to customers yet.[7]

The US Advantage

US petrochemical producers are ethane-based, not naphtha-dependent. They are insulated from the Hormuz disruption and are now surging exports to fill the gap left by idled Asian capacity. This is an ironic twist: the US is benefiting from a geopolitical supply shock while simultaneously engaged in a tariff war with the same countries it is now supplying.[5]

7. Oil Demand Destruction

This is the section that matters most for crude oil modelling. The petrochemical shutdown reduces crude volumes through two channels — but they are mechanistically different, and the distinction matters for forecasting:

  1. Direct feedstock loss (demand destruction): Naphtha crackers shut down because economics turn negative — $1,000/tonne naphtha makes cracker margins cash-negative. Crude demand disappears because buyers choose not to consume at current prices. This is genuine price-driven demand destruction.
  2. Refinery run cuts (supply disruption): Refiners cut throughput primarily because they cannot physically source Mideast crude — Hormuz is closed. This is a supply-side constraint forcing crude off the market, not a demand response. The refiner would run if the crude were available.

Decomposing the Crude Volume Impact

The total crude demand hit has two components — but they reflect different economic mechanisms:

  • Petrochemical feedstock loss (~0.5–1M b/d) — True demand destruction: Crackers shut down because naphtha economics turn negative at $1,000/tonne. Crude demand disappears price-driven — buyers could source the crude but choose not to, because the output (naphtha → ethylene) is cash-negative. This is the same mechanism as the cracker section: demand destruction.[6]
  • Refinery throughput cuts (~1.8–3M b/d) — Supply disruption, not demand destruction: Refiners are cutting runs primarily because Hormuz is physically closed — they cannot source Mideast crude at any price via the normal route. There is no adequate alternative supply at the required volume. This is a supply-side forced reduction. The same analytical distinction applied to the naphtha cracker section (supply disruption vs. demand destruction) applies here: refiners are not reducing consumption by choice; the supply chain has been severed.[8]

Net classification: True price-driven demand destruction is ~0.5–1M b/d (petchem channel). The larger 1.8–3M b/d refinery run-cut figure is supply-disruption-forced reduction — crude that would have been consumed if Hormuz were open. Both reduce crude throughput, but the mechanism matters for forecasting: supply disruption is geopolitically reversible; price-driven demand destruction may persist even after Hormuz reopens if cracker economics remain negative.

The Key Insight for Oil Modelling

The IEA's ~1M b/d year-average demand reduction for 2026 is the headline number, but it's misleading if applied to current conditions. That figure is diluted by Q1 2026 (pre-blockade) when demand was tracking normally. The acute March–April crude volume reduction of 2.8–4M b/d in Asia alone is the relevant figure for near-term crude market modelling.

Of that 2.8–4M b/d: approximately 0.5–1M b/d reflects true demand destruction (petchem crackers idled by negative margins); the remaining 1.8–3M b/d reflects supply-disruption-forced run cuts (refiners who cannot source Mideast crude through a closed Hormuz). The latter is geopolitically driven and more reversible than the headline "demand destruction" label implies.

If the Hormuz blockade persists through Q3, Hydrocarbon Processing estimates run cuts could reach 6M b/d — roughly 6% of global oil demand.[6]

9. Macro Implications

What This Means

  1. Oil price paradox: A supply chokepoint closure should be bullish for crude, but the demand destruction it triggers is partially offsetting. The net effect depends on duration — short blockade is net bullish, prolonged blockade tilts bearish as demand destruction compounds.
  2. Inflationary pulse in consumer goods: Plastic price increases (+20–100%) will flow through to food packaging, medical supplies, consumer products, and automotive within 2–3 months.
  3. Structural shift in petchem geography: US ethane-based producers gain permanent market share. Asian naphtha-based capacity that shuts down may never restart — the NDRC retirement schedule ensures it.
  4. China's consumption profile changes: 1.3M b/d of demand destruction is ~8% of China's normal throughput. If this persists, it reprices the entire forward curve for Chinese crude imports and weakens the "China demand recovery" thesis that has underpinned many commodity forecasts.
  5. Second-order demand destruction: Factories that can't get plastic feedstock idle their own operations — auto parts, electronics, packaging. This creates a multiplier effect on manufacturing activity and broader economic output.
