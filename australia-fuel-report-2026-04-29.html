3. From Crude to Your Car — The Supply Pipeline

Fuel takes 6–8 weeks from crude extraction to Australian petrol station. This lag is why we're still receiving fuel today that was secured before the crisis even started. Australia imports ~90% of fuel as refined product from Asian refineries — not directly from the Middle East. ✓ SBS News

5. Why Prices Haven't Gone Crazier — Demand Destruction

When oil hits $90–100/bbl, rich countries feel the pinch but can still pay. Poor countries literally cannot afford it and cut back. Their lost demand frees up supply for buyers who can pay — like Australia.

Bottom Line

Australian fuel is still affordable because of three compounding buffers:

  1. Pipeline lag — fuel arriving now was loaded pre-crisis (~6–8 week transit). You're still drinking from the pre-war pipeline.
    ✓ lastdrop.au shows fuel actively arriving; transit time confirmed by NRMA
  2. Demand destruction — poor countries priced out at $90–100 oil free up tanker capacity for wealthy buyers.
    ✓ Al Jazeera / IEA — Q2 2026 global demand down 1.5M bpd, deepest since COVID
  3. Government action — 762 ML~4.8M bbl reserve released, excise halved, 57 tankers underwritten, bilateral deals.
    ✓ SBS News, PM.gov.au, Canberra Times

⚠ The risk: These are all temporary buffers. If Hormuz stays closed past June, diesel goes critical first.

Why the Crude Pile-Up at Fujairah Is Actually a Good Sign

UAE has 4.8 mb/d of production capacity but its only export route while Hormuz is shut is the Habshan–Fujairah pipeline — physically capped at 1.5–1.8 mb/d. The gap (~2–3 mb/d) is going nowhere but Fujairah's tank farms, one of the world's largest oil storage hubs with >10 million barrels of capacity.

ScenarioUAE productionFujairah exportsGoing into Fujairah storage
Pre-crisis (OPEC quota) ~3.2 mb/d ~1.1 mb/d (Fujairah) + ~2.1 mb/d (Hormuz) ~0 (Hormuz was the main artery)
Now (Hormuz shut, OPEC quota) ~3.2 mb/d ~1.5 mb/d ~1.7 mb/d/day accumulating
Post-exit (full capacity) ~4.8 mb/d ~1.5–1.8 mb/d (pipe limit) ~3.0–3.3 mb/d/day accumulating
The silver lining: Every day Hormuz stays shut, UAE crude stockpiles at Fujairah. When Hormuz reopens — whether in weeks or months — that pre-positioned oil hits global markets immediately. No waiting for tankers to load from fields, no pipeline lag. It's sitting at the dock, ready to ship. This is why oil price spikes tend to crash fast when a chokepoint reopens: the pent-up supply flood is already staged.
For Australia specifically: Some of this Fujairah-stored UAE crude is already being sold to Asian refineries loading directly from Fujairah tanks. AU could be receiving UAE-origin crude-derived refined product sooner than traditional transit times suggest — but at elevated spot prices reflecting the storage premium. This is estimated; no public cargo-level data confirms the exact AU share.
