Hormuz Urea Disruption: Vector Understated It

The 25% figure in the original report is too conservative

✗ Figure incorrect — reality is worse
Vector's Claim
~25%
of global urea trade disrupted
Verified Reality
30–46%
depending on scope of disruption

The Arabian Gulf accounts for 46% of global urea production capacity. Qatar's QAFCO alone represents ~14% of global urea trade. UNCTAD puts the broad fertilizer complex (urea + ammonia + phosphate + sulphur) at 30% of global exports transiting the Strait.

The 25% figure likely assumes only Iranian exports are disrupted. A broader blockade affecting UAE, Qatar, and Saudi shipping raises the floor to 30–35%, not a ceiling.

Implication: The supply shock is structurally larger than the original report models. 2026 projections for food CPI — especially developing nation figures — should be revised upward.

+ Added: AUD Framework

AUD is in a structural conflict that makes it genuinely unusual. Normally food price spikes lift AUD via terms of trade — but this time Australia is on the cost side of the shock before the revenue side catches up.

Short-Term Headwinds (Now → Q3)

  • AUD already -3.1% in March 2026
  • Agricultural export value forecast -9% to A$73B (DAFF)
  • Farmer margin compression → reduced rural spending
  • 60–69% fertilizer imports from Hormuz zone → direct import cost shock

Medium-Term Tailwinds (Q4 → 2027)

  • If wheat prices re-rate upward, AU export pricing power recovers
  • Australia is a net food exporter — price taker on inputs, price setter on outputs
  • Southeast Asia / Middle East food demand for AU exports stays elevated
  • AUD historically re-correlates with ToT once supply shock prices in
Trade framing: Short-term AUD negative (cost shock arriving now). Medium-term the direction depends on whether wheat futures re-rate before Australian export volumes fall. The flip point is Q3 harvest data — if shortfall confirms, AUD could recover despite the input cost pain.
