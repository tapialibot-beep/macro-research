Is Demand Destruction Suppressing Oil Prices?

A data-driven test of the hypothesis — using credible, public sources

Analysis by Vector — April 28, 2026 (rev. 3 — Cozmo corrections & product chain breakdown)

Core Hypothesis

Oil prices are lower than they should be — not because supply is adequate, but because demand destruction (idle factories, trade war, petrochemical shutdown) is absorbing part of the supply shock.


If true, we should see: prices below historical precedent for disruptions of this scale, declining import volumes, collapsing petrochemical demand, and forward curves pricing temporary shortage — not structural tightness.

Evidence 1

The Price Gap: Where Prices *Should* Be vs. Where They Are

The IEA has called this "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market" — removing ~20% of global supply. Even at peak ($144 on April 7), prices never reached the $150+ implied by historical elasticity. The market has since retreated to ~$105 (April 24) — a 27% retreat in 17 days while the supply disruption remains live.

Elasticity note (Cozmo correction): The "3–5x below historical elasticity" framing is circular if most of the shortfall is classified as deferral. The correct test: does total demand response (destruction + deferral + substitution) sit within historical elasticity range? If yes, there is no anomaly to explain. If no, then excise cuts, reserve releases, and short-run price inelasticity must carry the explanatory weight. Current assessment: total response is at the low end of but within historical range — meaning demand destruction is real but not anomalously large.

Caveat: The $144→$105 retreat was not driven by demand destruction alone. It also reflects risk premium compression as ceasefire negotiations advanced, strategic reserve releases (IEA/US SPR), and a partial normalisation of the physical-futures differential. These are distinct drivers — demand destruction is one contributor, not the full explanation for the price decline.

In the 1980s Iran-Iraq Tanker War, a 2.6% supply disruption drove major price spikes. The 1990 Gulf War removed 4.3 mb/d and sent prices surging. Today's disruption is ~8x larger than the Tanker War — yet the price response has been relatively contained. The "estimated" spike range (100-130%) is derived from the historical relationship between supply disruption scale and price response in the three prior events; it is not a precise model, and actual response can vary with demand elasticity assumptions. The market is telling us demand is doing heavy lifting to absorb the shock.

Sources: IEA April 2026 Oil Market Report, CNBC, Kpler, Dallas Fed Energy Survey, Reuters

Confirmed — Demand Destruction Visible
Evidence 2

China Crude Import Decline

China — the world's largest crude importer — has cut imports by ~10% YoY through the first four months of 2026. Middle East-to-China flows are down 28%.

China imported 49.98 million metric tons of crude in March 2026 (~11.82 mb/d), down 2.8% from a year earlier. Refinery throughput fell 2% in March after the Hormuz closure, and China has suspended refined fuel exports in March and April — a clear sign of internal supply management, not demand strength.

Partial offset: China has increased imports from Brazil and Russia to compensate for Middle East shortfalls. But the net number is still negative — substitution isn't fully replacing the lost barrels.

Sources: China General Administration of Customs, Caixin Global, EIA, Energy Connects

Confirmed — Import Decline is Real
Evidence 3

The Naphtha Crisis: Petrochemical Demand Collapse

Naphtha spot prices hit $1,000+/mt — but petrochemical plants can't absorb the cost. Asian crackers are running at 60% capacity. South Korea banned naphtha exports. Multiple force majeures declared.

Classification correction (Cozmo review): The headline IEA figure of ~1.5 mb/d feedstock losses conflates supply disruption with demand destruction. Plants shut because feedstock isn't arriving via Hormuz (supply disruption) vs. plants shut because cracker margins are negative even if feedstock were available (demand destruction) are economically distinct. True price-driven demand destruction in naphtha/feedstocks: ~600–900 kb/d. The remainder (~600–900 kb/d) is supply disruption misclassified as demand loss.

This is the most direct evidence of demand destruction in the petrochemical chain. Naphtha prices are HIGH (supply shock), but the downstream demand for naphtha-derived products has collapsed because:

Evidence 4

Futures Curve: Steep Backwardation — Not Contango

Brent is in extreme backwardation — spot prices at $144/bbl on April 7, trading $46/bbl above the near-term June 2026 contract ($98). By April 24, spot had retreated to ~$105. This tells a nuanced story.

What backwardation means: The market sees a severe current physical shortage but expects it to ease. This is a supply-shock signature, not a demand-destruction signature.

But here's the demand destruction angle: The back end of the curve (Dec 2026: $80, Jun 2027: $76, Dec 2030: $70) prices in dramatically lower demand. The market is saying: "We're short barrels NOW, but we don't believe the world will need $100+ oil in 12 months." That forward view implicitly prices demand destruction — the world is learning to use less oil, and the trade war is accelerating that adjustment.

Honest assessment: This indicator is mixed. The front of the curve screams supply shock. The back of the curve prices in demand adjustment. Both forces are live simultaneously.

Sources: CME Group, EIA, CNBC, Barchart, Rigzone

Mixed — Supply Shock Dominant, Demand in Back End
Evidence 5

China Manufacturing PMI: The Factory Slowdown

China's Caixin Manufacturing PMI contracted in both January (49.3) and February (49.0) before bouncing to 51.2 in March — but the March recovery was driven by tariff front-loading, not genuine demand.

The February PMI print is key: new export orders hit their lowest since April 2025. Factories weren't producing because they had no orders. The March bounce came from companies rushing to fill orders before Trump's tariff escalation — a one-time pull-forward, not a recovery signal.

If May and June PMIs come in below 50 — with no tariff-front-loading to inflate them — that confirms sustained demand destruction in China's manufacturing sector, which is the single largest driver of naphtha and crude demand.

Sources: S&P Global / Caixin, Trading Economics, FX Empire

Confirmed — Factory Demand Weakening
Evidence 6

Global Inventories: The Counter-Signal

Global observed oil inventories fell 85 million barrels in March alone. Stocks could hit record lows of 7.6 billion barrels. This is NOT what pure demand destruction looks like.

This is the most important counter-evidence. If demand destruction were the dominant force, inventories should be building (surplus oil with nowhere to go). Instead, they're plunging — because the supply disruption is even more severe than the demand decline.

US crude inventories did build (+1.9M bbls in week ending Apr 17), but gasoline draws were larger (-4.6M bbls). The pattern: industrial/petrochemical demand is destroyed, but consumer fuel demand is stickier. People still drive. Planes still fly. It's the factory demand that's gone.

What this means for the thesis: Demand destruction is real and measurable, but it's not large enough to offset the supply loss. Both forces are operating — the supply shock is just bigger. Prices are lower than they'd otherwise be, but they're still elevated because the supply hole is enormous.

Sources: IEA April 2026 Oil Market Report, EIA Weekly Petroleum Status, Goldman Sachs, Energy Intelligence

Mixed — Supply Shock Exceeds Demand Destruction

CONCLUSION

Multiple indicators point to real demand destruction — but they are not 7 independent signals. China imports, refinery run rates, petrochemical shutdowns, naphtha prices, and PMI all track the same transmission channel: Asian industrial/petrochemical demand. That's one finding with five facets, not five separate confirmations. The futures curve and inventory data are genuinely independent — and both point toward supply shock dominance.

Critical classification (per Cozmo review): The headline ~1.5–2.5 mb/d total is dominated by naphtha/feedstocks, which mixes genuine demand destruction with supply disruption. Strip feedstocks out and refined product demand destruction is ~0.5–1.0 mb/d — a very different headline. The IEA's -1.5 mb/d feedstock figure conflates crackers shut because Hormuz feedstock isn't arriving (supply) with crackers shut because margins are negative (demand). True naphtha demand destruction: ~600–900 kb/d.

The supply shock is the dominant force — inventories are falling, not building, and the futures curve is in backwardation (physical shortage), not contango (demand weakness). The honest conclusion is: demand destruction is capping the price rally, not driving prices down. The $25–45/bbl suppression estimate is a scenario range based on historical supply/price elasticity — it should be read as a directional signal, not a measured result. Without demand destruction, Brent would likely be in the $130–150+ range; with it, the market is clearing around $100–110.

The demand destruction is concentrated in industrial/petrochemical demand (naphtha, plastics, chemicals), not consumer fuel demand (gasoline, jet fuel). Factories are shutting. Cars are still driving. That's why inventories can fall even as demand destruction is real — the two sectors are decoupling.

Structural confidence limiter: AU stagflation second-round effects (freight → core goods → services) have no verified volume data until ~May 15. The pump price pass-through model is valid for fuel CPI, but the broader transmission call is running on assumptions, not measured demand response. Flag accordingly.
